BEM. What does it look like?

<div class="block block__element block__element--modifier">
  That's a lot of classes!
</div>

.block__element--modifier
  background: #000
  color: #fff

Yes, those are double unscores and double hyphens. You'll learn to like them.

What's what?

  • Blocks are big "chunks" of your layout.
    headers, logos, navigation, articles
  • Elements are the pieces that go together to make that block.
    inputs, buttons, tabs
  • Modifiers are ways to provide alterations to elements.
    a blue button instead of a red one

SMACSS

SMACSS offers a pretty nice checklist for revamping your entire CSS. Complete in order:

  1. Base - Global reset
  2. Layout - Things that chunk your site into sections. Grid and such.
  3. Modules - Smaller parts of your site (calendar, nav, login panel, etc.). In BEM, these would be blocks.
  4. State - Hover states, etc.
  5. Theme - Specific to child themes your site might provide. Chances are you'll never need to do this.
  6. Fonts - Last, and perhaps wrongly considered least, fonts.

smacss.com/book/categorizing

Preprocessor placeholders aren't as amazing as we all hoped

Placeholders will let you assign some preprocessor code (CSS and functions) to a "placeholder". They aren't ran unless you explicitly call them, so you can make all kinds of little building blocks and combine them into actual objects at no cost to your CSS size. In fact, the @extend method saves you compiled (even gzipped) CSS by adding selectors to properties rather than reassigning all the properties of a selector.

Placeholders in action

$column
  float: left
$half
  width: 50%
$quarter
  width: 25%

.columns-2
  @extend $column, $half
.columns-4
  @extend $column, $quarter

// returns
.columns-2, .columns-4 {
  float: left;
}
.columns-2 {
  width: 50%;
}
.columns-4 {
  width: 25%;
}

Because modularity

Modularity is king. Everyone loves putting things together in ways that are tangible. We enjoy taking small pieces of stuff and piecing it together into something personal... You played with Legos when you were a kid. You may be a mechanic when you go home. You play with Legos now that you're an adult.

And there's a reason for it. We don't love Legos because of the HD graphics they provide. We love them because we can take little pieces and turn them into something nice fairly quickly and confidently.

The only thing that ever slowed us down was having to sift through the pieces to find the one you want. Before every big project I'd sort my Legos into color groups and then sizes.

That's what worked for me
